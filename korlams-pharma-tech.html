Pre-formulation Studies

The first step in drug development — understanding the drug's properties BEFORE formulating it

B.Pharm 4th Sem M.Pharm Important for Exams
💧 Solubility — The Foundation of Drug Development
💙 CORE CONCEPT
Solubility is simply "how well a drug dissolves in water or other solvents." If a drug doesn't dissolve, it won't absorb into the body — no matter how good the drug is. This is why solubility is the #1 property we study first!
🟣 Definition
Solubility is the maximum amount of drug (solute) that can dissolve in a given amount of solvent at a specific temperature to form a homogeneous solution.
USP Solubility Classification
TermParts of Solvent per Part of DrugWhat it means
Very SolubleLess than 1 partDissolves very easily
Freely Soluble1 to 10 partsDissolves well
Soluble10 to 30 partsNormal solubility
Sparingly Soluble30 to 100 partsSomewhat difficult
Slightly Soluble100 to 1000 partsDifficult to dissolve
Very Slightly Soluble1000 to 10,000 partsVery difficult
Practically InsolubleMore than 10,000 partsDoesn't dissolve
🟢 REAL EXAMPLE
Paracetamol — "Sparingly soluble" in water. That's why it takes some time to dissolve when you drop it in water! Aspirin is "Sparingly to slightly soluble" — this is why aspirin tablets can sometimes be hard on the stomach.
Methods to Improve Solubility
  • Micronization — Reduce particle size to increase surface area → more contact with solvent → faster dissolving
  • Solid Dispersions — Mix poorly soluble drug with water-soluble carrier (like PEG, PVP)
  • Salt Formation — Convert drug to its salt form (e.g., sodium salt) which is usually more soluble
  • Co-solvents — Add ethanol, propylene glycol to help drug dissolve
  • Complexation — Form cyclodextrin complexes to improve aqueous solubility
  • Nanosuspensions — Reduce drug to nano-size for dramatically improved dissolution
🌀 Dissolution — Drug Release from Dosage Form
💙 CORE CONCEPT
Dissolution is the process by which a solid drug dissolves into a liquid medium. It governs how fast the drug is absorbed. Even if solubility is good, slow dissolution = slow absorption!
Noyes–Whitney Equation
🟣 Formula
dC/dt = DA(Cs − C) / hV

Where: D = diffusion coefficient, A = surface area, Cs = saturation solubility, C = concentration in bulk, h = diffusion layer thickness, V = volume of dissolution medium
Factors Affecting Dissolution
  • Particle size — Smaller particles → more surface area → faster dissolution
  • Polymorphic form — Amorphous forms dissolve faster than crystalline
  • Temperature — Higher temperature increases dissolution rate
  • Viscosity of medium — Higher viscosity slows down dissolution
  • pH of medium — Affects ionization, thus solubility and dissolution
🟢 REAL EXAMPLE
Aspirin tablets crush faster in warm water than cold water — temperature increases dissolution rate!
🔷 Polymorphism — Same Drug, Different Crystal Forms
🟣 Definition
Polymorphism is the ability of a drug substance to exist in more than one crystalline form (polymorph), having the same chemical composition but different physical properties.
Types of Polymorphs
TypeDescriptionExample
EnantiotropicOne form converts to another reversibly at a specific temperatureSulfur
MonotropicOne form is always more stable; conversion is irreversibleCarbamazepine
AmorphousNo crystal structure; highest energy, fastest dissolutionAmorphous indomethacin
Solvates/HydratesSolvent/water molecules incorporated in crystal latticeAmpicillin trihydrate
🟡 EXAM TIP
Ritonavir (HIV drug) failed market because stable polymorph II appeared unexpectedly, reducing bioavailability. A classic pharma disaster caused by polymorphism!
📈 Stability — Drug Shelf Life & Degradation
💙 CORE CONCEPT
Stability studies ensure the drug remains safe, effective, and of acceptable quality throughout its shelf life. ICH Q1A guidelines define stability testing requirements worldwide.
ICH Stability Conditions
Study TypeTemperatureHumidityDuration
Long-term25°C ± 2°C60% RH ± 5%12 months minimum
Intermediate30°C ± 2°C65% RH ± 5%6 months
Accelerated40°C ± 2°C75% RH ± 5%6 months
  • Hydrolysis — Water breaks drug bonds (esters, amides). Most common degradation route.
  • Oxidation — Oxygen degrades drug. Use antioxidants like BHA, BHT.
  • Photodegradation — Light breaks drug. Use amber containers.
  • Thermal degradation — Heat causes decomposition. Store in cool conditions.
🔵 Particle Size — Critical Physical Property
💙 CORE CONCEPT
Particle size affects dissolution rate, bioavailability, flow properties, and content uniformity. Smaller particles = more surface area = faster dissolution!
Particle Size Reduction Methods
  • Micronization — Reduces particles to 1–10 µm using fluid energy mills. Used for poorly soluble drugs.
  • Nanonization — Reduces to <1 µm using wet media milling or high-pressure homogenization.
  • Ball milling — Mechanical grinding using steel/ceramic balls.
  • Hammer milling — High-speed rotating hammers break particles.

⚗️ pKa & pH — Ionization & Absorption
🟣 Definition
pKa is the pH at which 50% of a drug exists in ionized form and 50% in unionized form. Unionized drugs absorb better through biological membranes.
Henderson–Hasselbalch Equation
💙 For Weak Acids
pH = pKa + log [A⁻]/[HA]
💙 For Weak Bases
pH = pKa + log [B]/[BH⁺]
🟢 REAL EXAMPLE
Aspirin (pKa 3.5) is a weak acid. In stomach (pH 1.5), it stays mostly unionized → good absorption in stomach. In intestine (pH 6.8), it ionizes more → reduced absorption. This is why aspirin works fastest on empty stomach!

Solutions & Suspensions

Liquid dosage forms — formulation, properties, and quality standards

B.Pharm 4th Sem M.Pharm
Suspending Agents Used
  • Natural gums — Acacia, Tragacanth, Xanthan gum
  • Synthetic polymers — CMC (Carboxymethylcellulose), HPMC
  • Clays — Bentonite, Veegum (Magnesium aluminium silicate)
🟢 EXAMPLE
Calamine Lotion = suspension of Calamine (zinc carbonate) and Zinc Oxide in aqueous medium with bentonite as suspending agent. Shake well before use — classic suspension!

Antacid suspension (like Milk of Magnesia) contains Mg(OH)₂ particles suspended in water.
Ideal Properties of a Suspension
  • Uniform distribution of particles on shaking
  • Settles slowly and is easily resuspended
  • Pourable consistency — not too viscous
  • Chemically stable and preservative-free or properly preserved
