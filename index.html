🔬 Pre-formulation Studies

The first step in drug development — understanding the drug's properties BEFORE formulating it

B.Pharm 4th Sem M.Pharm Important for Exams

💧 Solubility — The Foundation of Drug Development

🧠 Core Concept Solubility is simply "how well a drug dissolves in water or other solvents." If a drug doesn't dissolve, it won't absorb into the body — no matter how good the drug is. This is why solubility is the #1 property we study first!
📖 Definition Solubility is the maximum amount of drug (solute) that can dissolve in a given amount of solvent at a specific temperature to form a homogeneous solution.

USP Solubility Classification

TermParts of Solvent per Part of DrugWhat it means
Very SolubleLess than 1 partDissolves very easily
Freely Soluble1 to 10 partsDissolves well
Soluble10 to 30 partsNormal solubility
Sparingly Soluble30 to 100 partsSomewhat difficult
Slightly Soluble100 to 1000 partsDifficult to dissolve
Very Slightly Soluble1000 to 10,000 partsVery difficult
Practically InsolubleMore than 10,000 partsDoesn't dissolve
🟢 Real Example Paracetamol — "Sparingly soluble" in water. That's why it takes some time to dissolve when you drop it in water! Aspirin is "Sparingly to slightly soluble" — this is why aspirin tablets can sometimes be hard on the stomach.

Methods to Improve Solubility

  • Micronization — Reduce particle size to increase surface area → more contact with solvent → faster dissolving
  • Solid Dispersions — Mix poorly soluble drug with water-soluble carrier (like PEG, PVP)
  • Salt Formation — Convert drug to its salt form (e.g., sodium salt) which is usually more soluble
  • Co-solvents — Add ethanol, propylene glycol to help drug dissolve
  • Cyclodextrin Complexation — Trap drug inside cyclodextrin cavity → forms water-soluble complex
  • pH Adjustment — Weak acids dissolve better in basic pH; weak bases dissolve better in acidic pH
⭐ Remember for Exam BCS (Biopharmaceutics Classification System) classifies drugs based on Solubility + Permeability:
Class I = High solubility, High permeability (Best! Easy to formulate)
Class II = Low solubility, High permeability (Challenging — need solubility enhancement)
Class III = High solubility, Low permeability (Absorption is the problem)
Class IV = Low solubility, Low permeability (Most difficult — poor bioavailability)

🔷 Polymorphism — Same Drug, Different Crystal Forms

🧠 Core Concept Polymorphism means a drug can exist in multiple crystal forms — like ice, water, and steam are all H₂O but in different states. Different crystal forms (polymorphs) of the SAME drug can have VERY different solubility, stability, and bioavailability!
📖 Definition Polymorphism is the ability of a solid material to exist in more than one form or crystal structure (polymorph). Each polymorph has the same chemical composition but different molecular arrangement.
✅ Stable Polymorph
  • Lower solubility
  • Longer shelf life
  • Less bioavailable
  • More stable
  • Preferred for storage
⚡ Metastable Polymorph
  • Higher solubility
  • Shorter shelf life
  • More bioavailable
  • Can convert to stable form
  • Preferred for absorption
🟢 Famous Example Ritonavir (HIV drug) — In 1998, Ritonavir suddenly stopped working! Investigation found that the drug had converted from Form I (metastable, more soluble) to Form II (stable, less soluble) during storage. This is called "polymorphic transformation" — a multi-million dollar disaster that shut down the product temporarily!
⭐ Remember Methods to detect polymorphs: DSC (Differential Scanning Calorimetry), X-Ray Powder Diffraction (XRPD), IR Spectroscopy, Hot Stage Microscopy. Amorphous form = NO crystal structure = highest solubility but LEAST stable.

⚖️ pKa and pH — Ionization of Drugs

🧠 Core Concept Most drugs are either weak acids or weak bases. At different pH levels, they exist in either ionized form (charged) or unionized form (uncharged). The uncharged (unionized) form absorbs better through membranes — this is called the "pH-Partition Hypothesis."
Henderson-Hasselbalch Equation:

For Weak Acids: pH = pKa + log([A⁻]/[HA])
For Weak Bases: pH = pKa + log([B]/[BH⁺])
⭐ Rule of Thumb "Weak acids absorb better in acidic environment (stomach) — they stay unionized."
"Weak bases absorb better in basic environment (intestine) — they stay unionized."
Example: Aspirin (weak acid) absorbs in stomach; Morphine (weak base) absorbs in intestine.

💊 Tablet Formulation

Most commonly used dosage form — learn everything from excipients to coating

Most Important Heavy Exam Topic B.Pharm / M.Pharm

📦 Excipients in Tablets — What Goes Inside Besides the Drug?

🧠 Core Concept A tablet is NOT just the drug. It contains many "helper ingredients" called excipients. Each excipient plays a specific role — like actors in a movie, each has its own job!
Excipient TypeJob / FunctionExamples
Diluents/FillersAdd bulk (drug alone is too small to compress)Lactose, MCC, Starch
BindersHold tablet together (like glue)PVP, HPMC, Gelatin
DisintegrantsBreak tablet apart in body (like a bomb)Starch, SSG, Croscarmellose
LubricantsPrevent sticking to punch during compressionMagnesium Stearate, Talc
GlidantsImprove powder flowColloidal silica, Talc
ColorantsAppearance and identificationFD&C dyes, Titanium dioxide
Flavors/SweetenersTaste masking (chewable tablets)Aspartame, Menthol
⭐ Lubricant Warning! Magnesium Stearate (most common lubricant) is hydrophobic (repels water). Too much MgSt = tablet won't disintegrate properly = poor absorption! Rule: Use 0.25–0.5% for water-soluble drugs, max 1–2% typically. Also, blend lubricant LAST and for SHORT time — overmixing destroys tablet hardness.

⚙️ Granulation — Why Do We Granulate?

🧠 Core Concept Powder alone flows poorly and compresses unevenly. Granulation = converting fine powder into larger aggregates (granules). Think of it like making a snowball from loose snow — you need to compress and bind it together!

Types of Granulation

1. Wet Granulation (Most Common)

Add a liquid binder to powder → mix to form wet mass → granulate → dry → mill → tablet

  1. Mix drug + diluent + disintegrant (intragranular)
  2. Add binder solution (water, alcohol, PVP solution)
  3. Knead until wet mass forms (like dough)
  4. Pass through granulator screen
  5. Dry in oven or FBD (Fluid Bed Dryer)
  6. Mill to uniform granule size
  7. Add extragranular excipients + compress
✅ Advantages
  • Good for poorly flowing powders
  • Better content uniformity
  • Strong tablets
❌ Disadvantages
  • Time-consuming
  • Not for moisture-sensitive drugs
  • Heat-sensitive drugs can't use it

2. Dry Granulation (Slugging / Roller Compaction)

Compress powder WITHOUT liquid → break up → granulate. Used for moisture-sensitive or heat-sensitive drugs!

🧠 Two Methods: Slugging — Compress powder into large tablets (slugs) then break them up into granules.
Roller Compaction — Pass powder between rollers under pressure → ribbon → granules. More popular today.

3. Direct Compression (No Granulation!)

Mix all excipients + drug → directly compress into tablets. Simple, fast, cheap!

⭐ Key requirement: Need specially designed excipients with good flow and compressibility.
Examples: MCC (Avicel), Spray-dried Lactose, Di-Pac (compressible sugar).
Not suitable for poorly compressible drugs or drugs with low dose (content uniformity issues).

🎨 Tablet Coating — Putting a Coat on Your Tablet

🧠 Core Concept Coating = applying a thin layer over the tablet core. Like putting on a jacket! Different coatings serve different purposes — some protect the drug, some protect the stomach, some release drug slowly.
Coating TypePurposeMaterials UsedExample Drug
Sugar CoatingMask bitter taste, appearanceSucrose, gum arabic, talcOld aspirin tablets
Film CoatingProtection, appearance, swallowing easeHPMC, HPC, Eudragit EMost modern tablets
Enteric CoatingProtect from stomach acid — release in intestineCAP, HPMCP, Eudragit L/SOmeprazole, Pantoprazole
Modified ReleaseSlow/controlled drug releaseEudragit RS/RL, EthylcelluloseMetformin XR, Metoprolol XL
🟢 Why Enteric Coating for Omeprazole? Omeprazole is a proton pump inhibitor — it becomes inactive in acidic stomach pH. Enteric coating (pH-sensitive polymer Eudragit L100) stays intact in stomach (pH 1-2) but dissolves in intestine (pH >6). This is how the drug reaches the small intestine safely and then gets absorbed!

Problems in Film Coating

  • Picking/Peeling — Film lifts off from tablet — cause: poor adhesion, too much coating solution
  • Bridging — Coating fills in the tablet logo/debossing — cause: high coating viscosity
  • Orange peel effect — Rough surface like orange skin — cause: rapid drying, high spray rate
  • Twinning/Sticking — Tablets stick together — cause: over-wetting, insufficient coating
  • Color variation — Non-uniform color — cause: poor mixing, migration of dye

🧪 Capsule Formulation

Hard and soft gelatin capsules — formulation, filling and quality testing

🔵 Hard Gelatin Capsules vs Soft Gelatin Capsules

🧠 Simple Way to Remember Hard Gelatin Capsule (HGC) = Two-piece shell, filled with powder/pellets/granules. Like an empty container you fill yourself!

Soft Gelatin Capsule (SGC) = One-piece sealed shell, filled with liquid or semisolid. Like a liquid-filled chocolate praline!
FeatureHard Gelatin CapsuleSoft Gelatin Capsule
ShellTwo-piece (body + cap)One-piece sealed shell
FillPowder, pellets, granules, mini-tabletsLiquids, oils, semisolids
Gelatin contentPure gelatin (no plasticizer)Gelatin + plasticizer (glycerin, sorbitol)
Shell flexibilityRigid and brittleFlexible and soft
ManufacturingSeparate capsule fillingRotary die or plate process
Example drugsAmoxicillin, OmeprazoleVitamin E, Fish oil, Cyclosporine
⭐ Key Fact — Capsule Sizes HGC sizes: 000 (largest) → 00 → 0 → 1 → 2 → 3 → 4 → 5 (smallest)
Size 0 is most commonly used in pharmaceutical industry.
Size 000 can hold 1.37 mL; Size 5 can hold only 0.13 mL.

⚙️ Capsule Filling Machines

🧠 Concept There are 3 main types of HGC filling machines, categorized by how they fill powder into capsules.
  • Dosing Disc Type (e.g., Bosch GKF) — Powder is compressed into slugs using a dosing disc with holes. Most accurate. Good for free-flowing powders.
  • Tamping Pin Type — Pins tamp powder into cavities. Multiple tamps build up dose. Used for cohesive powders.
  • Auger Fill Type — Screw/auger pushes powder into capsule. Used for difficult-to-handle powders.
🟢 Example Macofar and Zanasi machines use tamping pin principle. Bosch GKF series uses dosating disc. MG2 uses intermittent motion with dosating cylinders.

🧫 Gelatin — The Shell Material

🧠 What is Gelatin? Gelatin is a protein derived from collagen (from bones and skin of animals — usually pigs and cows). It forms a firm gel at room temperature and melts when heated. This is why capsule shells dissolve in body temperature!
⭐ Important Type A Gelatin = from porcine (pig) collagen, acid-treated. Isoelectric point pH 8-9.
Type B Gelatin = from bovine (cow) collagen, alkali-treated. Isoelectric point pH 4.8-5.2.
Vegetarian/Vegan capsules use HPMC (hydroxypropyl methylcellulose) instead of gelatin!

🧴 Solutions, Syrups & Suspensions

Liquid dosage forms — formulation principles and quality aspects

💧 Solutions — The Simplest Liquid Form

📖 Definition A pharmaceutical solution is a clear, homogeneous liquid preparation containing one or more dissolved substances in a suitable solvent.
🧠 Key Concept In a solution, drug is completely dissolved → forms molecular/ionic dispersion → particle size less than 1nm → most bioavailable liquid form (no dissolution step needed!)

Types of Solutions

TypeSolventExample
Aqueous SolutionWaterNormal Saline, ORS
SyrupConcentrated sugar + waterParacetamol syrup, Cough syrup
ElixirWater + Ethanol + sweetenerPhenobarbitone elixir
SpiritMostly alcoholPeppermint spirit
TinctureAlcohol (15-80%)Iodine tincture, Opium tincture
LinimentOil or alcoholicTurpentine liniment
⭐ Syrup Concentration Official syrup = 66.7% w/w sucrose (850g sucrose in 450 mL water). High sugar = self-preserving (doesn't need separate preservative). Below 65% sucrose → preservative needed. Sorbitol can replace sucrose for diabetic patients.

☁️ Suspensions — Solid Particles Suspended in Liquid

🧠 Core Concept Suspension = fine solid drug particles dispersed (but NOT dissolved) in a liquid medium. Particle size 0.5–5 μm. Key challenge: particles tend to settle down (sedimentation) — we must prevent or control this!
📖 Stokes' Law — The Science of Sedimentation Rate of settling depends on:
• Particle size (↑ size = faster settling) → reduce particle size!
• Density difference (↑ difference = faster settling)
• Viscosity of medium (↑ viscosity = slower settling → add thickeners!)
v = d² (ρs - ρL) g / 18η

where: v=settling velocity, d=particle diameter, ρs=density of solid,
ρL=density of liquid, g=gravity, η=viscosity

Ideal Properties of a Good Suspension

  • Settles slowly and is easily redispersed on gentle shaking
  • Does not form a hard "cake" at bottom
  • Uniform particle size (200 μm - controlled)
  • Pours easily — correct viscosity
  • Chemically stable and preservative-free or properly preserved

Suspending Agents Used

  • Natural gums — Acacia, Tragacanth, Xanthan gum
  • Synthetic polymers — CMC (Carboxymethylcellulose), HPMC
  • Clays — Bentonite, Veegum (Magnesium aluminium silicate)
🟢 Example Calamine Lotion = suspension of Calamine (zinc carbonate) and Zinc Oxide in aqueous medium with bentonite as suspending agent. Shake well before use — classic suspension!

Antacid suspension (like Milk of Magnesia) contains Mg(OH)₂ particles suspended in water.

💉 Sterile Products — Injections & Ophthalmic Preparations

Products that MUST be free from microorganisms — critical quality requirements

Very Important Safety-Critical

🔑 Why Sterility Matters — The Fundamentals

🧠 Core Concept Sterile = completely FREE from all living microorganisms and their spores. When you inject something directly into blood (IV) or tissues (IM), it bypasses all body defenses. Even ONE bacterium can cause a life-threatening infection or sepsis. This is why sterile manufacturing is the most strictly regulated area in pharma!
⭐ 5 Requirements of Parenteral Products 1. Sterility — Free from microorganisms
2. Apyrogenicity — Free from pyrogens (fever-causing substances)
3. Freedom from visible particles
4. Isotonicity — Same osmotic pressure as blood (~308 mOsmol/kg)
5. Correct pH — Usually 4-9 (blood pH 7.4)

🔥 Methods of Sterilization — How to Kill Microorganisms

MethodHow it WorksUse ForKey Parameter
Autoclaving (Moist Heat)Steam under pressure kills bacteriaHeat-stable aqueous solutions121°C, 15 psi, 15-20 min
Dry HeatOxidation/denaturation by hot airOils, powders, glassware160°C/2hr or 170°C/1hr
FiltrationPhysical removal through membraneHeat-sensitive drugs (antibiotics)0.22 μm filter (absolute)
Gamma RadiationDNA damage by ionizing radiationDry powders, medical devices25 kGy minimum dose
ETO (Ethylene Oxide)Alkylates DNA and proteinsHeat-sensitive equipmentToxic — residue testing needed
UV RadiationDNA damage (thymine dimers)Air, surfaces onlyLow penetration — surface only
⭐ D-value & Z-value D-value = Decimal Reduction Time = time needed to kill 90% of microorganisms at a given temperature.
Z-value = temperature change needed to change D-value by 10-fold.
F0-value = sterilizing equivalent at 121°C (for autoclaving). F0 ≥ 12 min typically required.

🌡️ Pyrogens — The Silent Danger

🧠 What Are Pyrogens? Pyrogens are fever-producing substances — mainly Lipopolysaccharides (LPS) from gram-negative bacterial cell walls. They're NOT killed by regular sterilization! A solution can be sterile BUT still have pyrogens. Pyrogens cause fever, chills, hypotension, and can lead to septic shock!
🔬 LAL Test (Limulus Amebocyte Lysate)
  • Uses horseshoe crab blood
  • Detects endotoxins (LPS)
  • Sensitive and fast
  • In vitro test
  • Most common today
🐇 Rabbit Pyrogen Test
  • Inject drug into rabbits
  • Measure temperature rise
  • Detects all pyrogens
  • In vivo — animal test
  • Being replaced by LAL
⭐ Depyrogenation To remove pyrogens from glassware: Dry Heat 250°C for 30 min (endotoxin destruction).
To remove from liquids: Filtration with special filters, activated charcoal, or reverse osmosis.
Regular autoclaving does NOT remove pyrogens — it only kills bacteria!

📊 Stability Testing — ICH Guidelines

How long can a drug stay effective? Stability testing answers this question

📅 What is Stability Testing?

🧠 Core Concept Stability = the ability of a drug product to remain within its specification (potency, purity, appearance) throughout its shelf life. Stability testing = deliberately stressing the product under various conditions to predict how long it will remain effective and safe. Like checking how fast milk spoils under different temperatures!
📖 ICH Definition of Shelf Life Shelf life (expiry date) = the time period during which a drug product is expected to remain within the approved specification when stored under defined conditions.

🌡️ ICH Q1A — Stability Testing Conditions

⭐ ICH Climatic Zones Zone I — Temperate (Germany, UK): 21°C/45% RH
Zone II — Mediterranean/Subtropical (USA, Japan): 25°C/60% RH
Zone III — Hot & Dry (Iraq, Iran): 30°C/35% RH
Zone IVa — Hot & Humid (India, Brazil): 30°C/65% RH
Zone IVb — Hot & Very Humid (SE Asia): 30°C/75% RH
Study TypeConditionDurationPurpose
Long-term (Real-time)25°C / 60% RH (Zone II)12-60 monthsConfirm shelf life
Intermediate30°C / 65% RH6-12 monthsFor Zone II products
Accelerated40°C / 75% RH6 monthsPredict shelf life faster
Stress TestingHigher temp, light, acid/baseVariesIdentify degradation products
🧠 Arrhenius Equation — The Science Behind Accelerated Testing Higher temperature = faster chemical reactions = faster degradation. Using Arrhenius equation, we can predict room temperature shelf life from accelerated 40°C data. Rule of thumb: For every 10°C increase, reaction rate doubles (Q10 rule). So 40°C data can predict 25°C shelf life in much shorter time!

⚗️ Degradation Pathways — How Drugs Degrade

PathwayWhat HappensExample DrugsPrevention
HydrolysisDrug reacts with water → breaks chemical bondsAspirin → salicylic acid; esters, amides, lactamsReduce moisture, use anhydrous formulation
OxidationDrug reacts with oxygen → loses electronsVitamin C, morphine, adrenaline, oilsAntioxidants (BHA, BHT, Na metabisulfite), nitrogen purge
PhotolysisLight energy breaks drug bondsNifedipine (turns brown), riboflavin, tetracyclineAmber containers, light-protective packaging
RacemizationDrug converts between optical isomersAdrenaline, pilocarpineControl pH and temperature
PolymerizationDrug molecules link together into large polymersInsulin, formaldehyde from methenamineRefrigeration, proper pH
🟢 Classic Example — Aspirin Hydrolysis Aspirin (acetylsalicylic acid) + Water → Salicylic acid + Acetic acid.
The vinegar smell from old aspirin tablets = acetic acid = sign of degradation!
This is why aspirin tablets should be stored dry, in a cool place, and discarded if they smell like vinegar.
⭐ Antioxidants to Remember For oil-based preparations: BHA (Butylated Hydroxyanisole), BHT (Butylated Hydroxytoluene), α-Tocopherol
For aqueous preparations: Sodium metabisulfite, Ascorbic acid, Sodium ascorbate
Chelating agents (prevent metal-catalyzed oxidation): EDTA (Ethylenediaminetetraacetic acid)
